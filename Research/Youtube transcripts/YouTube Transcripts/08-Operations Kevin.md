# Operations Kevin: Execution. 
**Source:** [How I Built a SaaS Startup (From Idea to Revenue)] (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=180KJQryGwc)
**Key Takeaways:** Kevin built his own business and here he takes us trough his experience and what values he wants to share for others B2B business as well.
* *Identifying the Problem:* After transitioning from corporate roles at Amazon and Apple to running a freight company, Kevin Park realized he lacked efficient tools for route and driver scheduling. He identified a significant gap in the market for automated logistics software.
* *Developing the Solution:* Kevin collaborated with his co-founder, Moses, to develop an algorithm capable of automatically managing complex scheduling variables—such as driver availability and truck constraints—which previously took him hours to handle manually.
* *Building the MVP:* The team moved from a rudimentary proof-of-concept to building a functional dashboard. They focused on proving that the logic worked before expanding the team, eventually recruiting a designer, Jeremy, to improve the user interface and overall experience.
* *Scaling and Commitment:* The startup, Laminar Co-Pilot, has successfully transitioned from an initial idea to a revenue-generating product. The team is now focused on refining their backend infrastructure to scale operations and reach a broader customer base.

I felt like a free man escaping the 9-to-5, and my business was growing fast. We were hiring drivers left and right, adding more trucks to our fleet weekly, but before I knew it, I was working 60 hours a week to run this company. One thing I missed from my corporate 9-to-5 was having access to the best tools and software that money can buy. This is the story of how Laminar Co-Pilot started a year ago during a random car ride to Santa Barbara.
The first time we talked about Co-Pilot was actually during our drive up to Santa Barbara. So we had a cycling event, and on the way up we were just talking about business ideas, and one of the business ideas he had was to have some sort of app for the company that he runs. We both thought there was a lot of potential, and so yeah, that's kind of the first time we talked about Laminar Co-Pilot.
That entire morning I had actually spent scheduling my team for my freight company. Even though I was coming from all this experience in operations, I was still having to do a lot of this manually—a lot of Excel, a lot of spreadsheets, and a lot of, you know, just manual maneuvers until you can make it work. And I knew that there had to be some sort of tool that can do this automatically for me, but when I Googled it, I found none. And that was a complete shocker to me. And that's when I realized, hey, there's something here, and potentially if we can code this, it could be huge.
Imagine this: you have X number of drivers, and let's just say you have Y number of trucks, and every week you want to fulfill Z number of routes optimally and efficiently. In theory, you can just go down this list and assign each driver to a truck, but what happens when you add different variables to the story? Suddenly a driver only wants to work Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday, and let's just say this truck is actually out on a trip and only becomes available Monday through Thursday. Now what happens if you have Z number of routes but not enough trucks? On that drive up to Santa Barbara, I looked at Moses and I asked him, is it possible to create an algorithm that can do all of this for us automatically?
The first step, I think, to writing great software is just having the context necessary to write the software—understanding the problem, right? You just figure things out as you go, and there's always going to be roadblocks that come up, but step-by-step that moves the process along and allows you to iterate and build something that ends up being used.
