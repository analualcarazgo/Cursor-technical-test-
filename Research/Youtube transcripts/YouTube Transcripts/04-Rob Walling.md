# 04-Rob Walling: Conversion and Micro-SaaS Strategy
**Source:** [7 CRUCIAL Things Every SaaS Landing Page Should Have (with examples)] (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cJ10BYWNPns)
**Key Takeaways:** Rob Walling tells us that conversion isn't just about "tricks", it's about reducing friction in the user journey and clearly communicating the value proposition of your product. In B2B this is really important.
* *Friction is a conversion killer:* Every extra field in a sign up form or confusing navigation step decreases the likelihood of a user becoming a paying customer.
* *The "Stair-Step" approach:* Don't try to build the next Facebook, focus on solving small, specific problems for a niche audience to ensure your product has actual demand.
* *UX is about trust:* If your site looks unprofessional or the user flow is broken, users will subconsciously assume your product is also broken.
* *Data-informed, not data-driven:* While analytics are important, you must combine them with qualitative feedback—talk to your users to understand "why" they are leaving.
**Relevance:** Walling’s advice is perfect for transitioning into UX/CX. He provides a pragmatic framework for aligning business goals (conversion) with user needs (UX), which is exactly what we need for the virtual therapeutic collective project.

## RAW TRANSCRIPT
If you're looking to launch a SaaS, you need a landing page to send potential customers to, but a landing page isn't just a generic website. It has a job, and that job is to convince people to pay you money to use your product. As your business grows, your landing pages will get more and more sophisticated, and eventually, you may need to have multiple landing pages for the same product.
For a simple pre-launch landing page, you need three key ingredients: a headline, a sub-headline, and a call to action. Your headline should be a prominent sentence that immediately explains the problem your product solves and for whom. Don't get too long or complicated; the goal is to be compelling. The lazy way is to simply say what your product is, but you want to focus on the value—does it save time, money, or effort? Spend time on variations, play with humor, and aim for curiosity to keep visitors on the page.
The sub-headline expands on the headline. Use it to explain your product in natural language, tell people how you’re different from alternatives, and expand on what makes you awesome. Finally, the call to action (CTA) tells people exactly what to do next, whether it’s signing up for an email list or starting a trial. Crucially, these three elements must be visible above the fold, with no scrolling required, to reduce distractions and keep the user focused.
Once you have a product live, you should add more elements to improve conversion. Screenshots or mockups are essential to give users a preview of what they’re signing up for. You can go from simple screenshots to more complex assets like Loom walkthroughs or interactive homepage previews. Furthermore, social proof is non-negotiable to earn trust. This can range from displaying customer logos to embedding social media posts where users talk about your product, or even full-blown text and video testimonials. If you have case studies, link to them directly to provide deep validation.
Additionally, always include a link to your pricing page. Folks are used to seeing this on SaaS websites, and if it's missing, they are likely to bounce. Even if you don't have public pricing, use the page to guide users toward a demo request. Below the fold, you should list your benefits and features in depth, keeping the copy succinct but informative.
As you refine the page, keep in mind that white space is a designer's best tool—it adds scannability and prevents the page from feeling crowded. Add multiple CTAs throughout long pages so users don't have to scroll back up to sign up. Limit outbound links to prevent users from leaving your site, and keep form fields to an absolute minimum to reduce hurdles. Finally, ensure your site is fast-loading, mobile-optimized, and connected to tracking tools so you can iterate based on actual data. Your landing page will never be perfect; launch with the basics and treat it as a living document.
