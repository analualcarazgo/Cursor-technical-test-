# 03-David Lieb (Y Combinator): Strategy and User Centric design.
**Source:** [How To Keep Your Users | Startup School] (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VNxBZ7ka5J0)
**Key Takeaways:** In this video, he breaks down how to build products that users love by focusing on solving singular, painful problems before trying to scale features.
* *Obsess over the problem:* Don't start with a "cool" feature. Start by identifying a real, painful problem that users face every single day.
* *Simplicity as a feature:* If your product is too complex to explain in seconds, you've already lost. Friction is the enemy of adoption.
* *Feedback loops:* Building isn't a one way street; you need to get your prototype in front of real people as soon as possible to see where it breaks.
* *Scalability:* Don't over engineer for 100 million users on day one. Focus on making 100 people absolutely love what you've built.
**Relevance:** Lieb proves that "user experience" is actually just the sum of how well you solve a specific frustration. For any project we build, this is our north star thinking about B2B SaaS.

## RAW TRANSCRIPT
Hi everyone, I'm David Lieb, and I am a group partner here at YC. YC has a famously simple motto: "Make something people want." I think it's the purest statement of the job of startup founders, and we talk about this a lot. But what gets talked about a lot less is how do you know if you've actually done it? Did we make something people want? Today, I want to go deep on the single best way that I have found to answer this question and do it in a really quantitative way: cohort retention.  
High-level cohort retention is the idea of tracking what fraction of your new users keep using your product over time. This isn't a new concept; there's actually a ton of content on the internet about cohort retention, and it's even built into most analytics tools that you might be using. But I bet that you don't fully understand what it measures and how to interpret the numbers. The reason I suspect that you don't understand it is that I didn't understand it in my startup until many years later, when it was nearly too late. I remember one specific moment when I was pitching a very prestigious VC firm for our Series A; they asked me, "Hey Dave, how's your cohort retention?" I gave them a very hand-wavy answer, and after the meeting, I realized that what I said must have made absolutely no sense. I hope that I can save you from that mistake with this video.
Before we dive in, a quick background on me: I did YC back in the summer of 2009 with my startup, Bump. Bump let you share contact information and photos with other people by literally bumping your phones together. Bump was one of the first mobile apps to reach more than 100 million users, but it ultimately didn't work as a business. Thankfully, we did a few pivots into the photo-sharing and photo-management space and were ultimately acquired by Google. Our last app, a photo-management app, formed the basis for Google Photos, which I then worked on for nearly a decade. Google Photos today serves well more than a billion users, so I've made something that a lot of people want, but I've also learned the very hard way how to know when you have not yet made something that people want.  
The key insight, and the reason it's called cohort retention, is that we're going to track individual groups of new users—or cohorts—over time, instead of trying to look at the user base mixed together. This gives you a much better sense of how individual users continue to use your product over time. To do this, we have to define three things: how we isolate the cohorts, what action counts a user as active, and which time period we want to measure. The most common way to isolate cohorts is by when they first used your product, like grouping new users by week or month. As you get more advanced, you can slice by other dimensions like country, region, or acquisition channel.
The action is how you qualify a user as active. The simplest way is just opening an app, but a better way is to pick a specific feature that correlates with real value, like posting a photo or using a specific workflow in your B2B tool. For Google Photos, we chose whether a user tapped in and viewed a photo full-screen. You want to filter away things where a user might be touching the product but not getting real value. Finally, you have to define the time period—the granularity of your measurement. This should match the intended usage frequency of your product. If you're a social app, measure daily; if you're a utility, weekly might be better; if you're a travel app, quarterly or annually might make more sense.
The core insight behind cohort retention curves is that the only thing that matters is whether your curves get flat. The shape of the curve is what matters, not the absolute number. If your curves don't flatten out, you're on a treadmill, losing old users as fast as you acquire new ones. If you can get them to flatten, even at a small percentage, you have a chance to accumulate a large customer base over time. When things go south, you'll want to know quickly, so I recommend refreshing these graphs every week or two. If your retention isn't good, you can improve it by refining the product, targeting better users, improving onboarding, or fostering network effects. Ultimately, the best of the best are cohort curves that don't just flatten, but actually go up over time. If you see a "layer cake" chart where your total active users are growing because old cohorts are sticking around, congratulations—you're off to the races.
